Firstly, it is clearly visible in this plot that the majority of the houses in this data set are less than 60 years old. Secondly, the distribution is clearly multimodal. Many houses that were built between the present and 30 years ago (1990) and in the period between roughly 65 years and 40 years ago (1955 to 1970) are included in this data set. Smaller peaks are visible at 80 years old and 95 to 100 years old.

The distribution is extremely right skewed as well, as there are a few houses included that were constructed more than 100 years ago. This was expected, as old houses are usually demolished and replaced by newly constructed houses, which cannot be younger than when this data set was created. Observing left skewed data would therefore have been very unusual.

The graph shows boxplots of the house prices in the neighborhoods of Ames, arranged by their median. I choose median for the graph and to determine the most expensive and least expensive neighborhood, because the mean could be influenced by a possible skew in the prices of certain neighborhoods. Median would not be influenced by such skew. The first two tibbles, however, show that both mean and median provided the same results for the most and least expensive neighborhoods.

The most expensive neighborhood is Stone Brook (StoneBr), with a median house price of $340,692 (and a mean of 339,316 dollars).

The least expensive neighborhood is Meadow Village (MeadowV) with a median house price of just $85,750 (and a mean of 92,947 dollars).

To discover the most heterogeneous neighborhood (most variation in housing price), I used the variance, because the variance indicates how much the values (prices) differ from each other and from the mean. I could also have used the standard deviation, as that is simply the square root of the variance. The results are the same. The most heterogeneous neighborhood is Stone Brook (StoneBr) with a variance of 15242150036 (thus a standard deviation of $123,459.1 which by itself is more than the average housing price of Meadow Village).

I used Bayesian Model Averaging to determine the Best Predictive Model. BMA does not use one model, but averages multiple models, which can be used for prediction. As I have to find only one model that is best for predicting the natural log of home prices, I used the BMA model to find the Best Predictive Model. The BMA used the prior BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion). This chooses models with the best goodness of fit and introduces a penalty for the number of parameters in the model. This produces a parsimonious model with statistically significant parameters. Estimating the BPM provides the model with predictions closest to that of the full BMA.

Curiously, the best model for predicting the natural log of the home prices is the same as the full model. Therefore, the final model includes Lot.Area, Land.Slope, Year.Built, Year.Remod.Add and Bedroom.AbvGr.
